Beaches

When you need to take a break from the fast-paced excitement of the big City like Madrid look no further than Spain’s Costa del Sol (Coast of the Sun) peninsula. The coast of Malaga offers visitors with more than 10 kilometers of tranquil oases for those seeking to relax and unwind. There are numerous beach bars, restaurants, and promenades. The easiest and most popular beach is La Malagueta due to its central location and proximity to public transportation. Other amenities include hammocks, beach rentals, on-duty life guards, shower facilities, and floating platforms. For those seeking an authentic fresh fish platter, head to Fábrica de Cemento Beach, which is known as a fisherman’s town and has an array of restaurants serving "fritura malagueña" or Malaga fried fish platter. The dish consists of the “catch of the day” such as anchovies, squid, red mullet, or mackerel that are then fried in olive oil. Those seeking an upscale resort backdrop should head should head east to Marbella, the playground of the rich and famous – Prince, the Artist who passed away in April of 2016, once owned a Spanish Villa here. The best known beach here is Puerto Banus, which also has a marina and myriad of bars and restaurants.

Gibraltar

For a fun getaway from Spain visitors can take a guided tour from Malaga to the British territory of Gibraltar. The distance is only 1.5 hours from Malaga. Visitors will feel as though they have been transported to England with the array of pubs, tea and scones, the British military on guard, and famous red telephone boxes. Take a cable car ride to the “Top of the Rock” and visit the residential Barbary macaque monkeys. Be sure to keep all foods item well stored as these furry creatures have quite the appetite. From the summit, visitors can view the Mediterranean Sea both Africa’s Moroccan coast and Spain’s Costa del Sol. In case you don’t have enough souvenirs from Madrid, Seville, and Malaga there are plenty of duty-free shops to stock up on liquor, watches, and fragrances. Other activities include whale watching and cave exploration. Be sure to pack your passport and enjoy the day.

City Exploration

City Exploration is one of the oldest cities in existence and still has remains from the Romans that once ruled this area more than 2,000 years ago. No need to make the trip to Rome to see the amphitheater, as there one right here and admission is free. The City of Malaga was not only influenced by the Romans but the Moors also once called this home as evidenced by the Alcazaba fortress palace. This example of Moorish influence was built around 1050 A.D. by the Hammudid dynasty. Although not as extensive as the Alhambra in Granada, there are still mosaic tiles, dungeons, and towers for visitors to enjoy. There are also beautiful gardens for strolling as well as stunning views of the Roman amphitheater below and the stunning port where cruise ships docks. Lovers of art won’t want to miss the exhibits of Malaga’s favorite son, Picasso. There are more than 200 pieces of art on display at the Museo Picasso. There are other works showcasing Roman, Islamic, and Phoenician cultures. Price of admission is €10 euros and the museum is open most days from 10:00am to 6:00pm year round.
